The pipeline

Six steps from stalled to safely sent.

Risk is decided by replayable code. The model only writes — inside a bounded contract — and a human signs off before anything irreversible happens.

Auto-advancing — click any step to explore.
Step 01 · Deterministic

Triage

Find who's stuck — and exactly how badly. A transparent risk score and the single blocking step, every time.

HowA deterministic risk score plus the blocker, from an auditable formula. No model touches this call — it's replayable and identical on every run.
Output: risk level + blocker
Step 02 · Deterministic

Diagnose

Read the engagement state, name the root cause, and pick a reactivation play that varies by why they're stuck — not just by which step they're on.

HowEngagement state from recency × steps × tenure; merchant-vs-platform blocker source; a play matched to the cause, not a one-size message.
Output: root cause + play
Step 03 · Bounded LLM

Draft

Now — and only now — a model writes the message. It works inside a schema-constrained contract and declares every claim it makes.

HowA bounded LLM produces structured output with a typed claims array. The untrusted merchant name never enters the prompt — it's substituted after gating.
Output: draft + declared claims
Step 04 · Claims gatekeeper

Gate

Before a human ever sees it, every declared claim is checked against the merchant's own data — and against forbidden-claim guardrails.

HowEach declared claim must trace to merchant data; forbidden claims are blocked outright. Anything unverifiable is held, not sent.
Output: pass / hold / block
Step 05 · Deterministic eval

Score

Measure the draft's quality with graders that run the same way every time — so quality is a number, not a vibe.

HowDeterministic graders for structure, state-consistency, and policy. Corrupted-record cases have teeth — a bad draft fails loudly.
Output: graded scores
Step 06 · Human-in-the-loop

Approve

A person makes the final call — hold, reject, or send. Every decision lands in a full audit trail, beside a fail-closed cost ledger.

HowThe human owns the irreversible action. Sends are simulated in this demo; spend is hard-capped at $5 and recorded line by line.
Output: decision + audit record

Why a marketplace exec can trust it

A direct antidote to the AI-outreach failure frontier.

False claims, churn from spammy blasts, regulatory liability — the known failure modes of "let AI message customers." This system is designed against each one.

Deterministic where it counts

The risky call — who's at risk and why — is made by code, not a model. Same inputs, same output, every replay.

Replayable by design

The AI is bounded, never autonomous

The model only writes, inside a schema, with declared claims. It can't decide, can't act, and can't send.

Constrained output only

Every declared claim traces to data

The gatekeeper checks each claim against the merchant's own record, and blocks forbidden claims outright.

No unverifiable promises

A human owns every irreversible send

Nothing leaves the system without a person choosing to send it. The default is to hold, not to act.

Human-in-the-loop gate

Spend is hard-capped at $5

A fail-closed cost ledger records every model call. Hit the cap and the system stops — it never overspends quietly.

Why it generalizes

The same spine works far beyond merchant activation.

Deterministic triage → bounded LLM → claims-gate → eval → human approval → audit. Any problem shaped like "use AI to act on people or accounts at scale, safely" can stand on it.
