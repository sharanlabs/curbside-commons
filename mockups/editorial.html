01 — The problem

The long tail of merchants who signed up and never went live.

Every marketplace has them: businesses that created an account, started onboarding, and then quietly stopped. They are not rejections — they are unfinished revenue. Each stalled merchant is GMV that was almost won and then left on the table, and the tail is far too long to work by hand, one account at a time.

Lost revenue, not lost leads

Signed-up-but-never-live merchants are real GMV the marketplace already paid to acquire — and then never collected.

Too big to work by hand

The long tail outnumbers the team. Manual reactivation reaches the loudest accounts and misses the rest.

They stall mid-setup

Verify business → menu → photos → hours → banking → final check. Most drop somewhere in that chain, each for a different reason.

Generic outreach fails

The number-one reason reactivation flops: a one-size message that ignores the merchant's actual blocker.

Outreach doesn't fail because no one wrote. It fails because the message wasn't matched to why they stopped.

The thesis of this report
02 — The method

Six steps, one spine. The risky calls stay deterministic; the writing stays bounded.

Each merchant travels the same audited path — from a replayable score to a human decision. Nothing irreversible happens without a person at the end of it.

1

Triage

Find who's stuck — and how badly.

A deterministic risk score and named blocker, from an auditable formula. The same inputs always produce the same call, so any decision can be replayed and defended.

2

Diagnose

Pin the exact reason they stopped.

Engagement state plus root cause, mapped to a reactivation play that varies by why they're stuck — a verification problem and a banking problem are not the same conversation.

3

Draft

Write the right next message.

A bounded, schema-constrained LLM produces the outreach within fixed limits. The model writes the prose; it never decides the score, the blocker, or whether to send.

4

Gate

Check every declared claim against the data.

A claims-gatekeeper traces each declared claim back to the merchant's own record and enforces forbidden-claim guardrails — so the message can't assert something the data doesn't support.

5

Score

Measure draft quality, deterministically.

Eval graders check structure, state-consistency, and policy — so quality is a measured number with regression teeth, not a vibe.

6

Approve

A human decides: hold, reject, or send.

The irreversible step belongs to a person. Every choice lands in a full audit trail, beside a $5 fail-closed cost ledger that stops the run before it can overspend.

04 — Why it's safe

The AI is the writer, never the decider.

This is a direct antidote to the failure frontier of AI outreach — false claims, churn, and regulatory liability. The design pushes every risky decision out of the model and into code, data, and a person.

Deterministic where it counts

Code makes the risky call — the score and the blocker. Same inputs, same output, every time. Replayable, and defensible.

Bounded & evaluated, never autonomous

The LLM works inside a fixed schema and is graded by deterministic evals. It drafts; it does not act.

Every claim traces to data

The gatekeeper ties each declared claim to the merchant's own record. If the data doesn't back it, the claim doesn't ship.

A human owns the send

The one irreversible action — sending — always belongs to a person. Hold, reject, or send.

Spend is hard-capped

A $5 cost ledger fails closed — it halts the run before it can overspend. The public demo is replay-only: no live calls.

A full audit trail

Every step, score, and decision is recorded. The run can be reconstructed end to end — by an auditor, not just an engineer.

05 — Why it generalizes

The spine outlives the use case.

Stalled-merchant activation is the worked example. The shape underneath it is a pattern for any time you want AI to act on people or accounts at scale — safely.

Deterministic triage→ bounded LLM→ claims-gate→ eval→ human approval→ audit

Swap the merchants for patients, borrowers, students, or subscribers and the discipline holds: let code make the risky call, let the model write within bounds, prove every claim, measure the output, and put a person on the trigger. That is the transferable part — and the reason this is built to be adopted, not just demoed.
