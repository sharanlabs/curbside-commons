01 — The stall

A merchant signed up, got halfway, and went quiet.

Every marketplace has a long tail of them — businesses that opened an account, started onboarding, and then stopped. They are not rejections; they are unfinished revenue, GMV that was almost won and then left on the table. The tail is far too long to work by hand, and a one-size message ignores the very thing that matters: why each one is stuck.

Lost revenue, not lost leads

Signed-up-but-never-live merchants are real GMV the marketplace already paid to acquire — and then never collected.

They stall mid-setup

Verify business → menu → photos → hours → banking → final check. Most drop somewhere in that chain, each for a different reason.

Generic outreach fails

The number-one reason reactivation flops: a message that ignores the merchant's actual blocker.

And AI can make it worse

Hand the writing to a model and it will help — until it confidently states something the data never supported. That is the risk this system is built against.

The danger isn't that AI won't write. It's that it will write something true-sounding that the data can't back.

03 — Why two checks, not one

One check proves what's declared. The other reads what's written.

Generic AI guardrails check tone and policy. They rarely check whether a sentence is true to the data. ActivationOps runs both directions — and it's the second that catches the Friday line.

forward

The claims-gatekeeper

Deterministic, and first in line. Each claim the draft declares is traced forward to a specific field in the merchant's record — and forbidden-claim guardrails are enforced. If a declared claim has no field behind it, the gate blocks it.

claim "2 of 5 steps" → steps_completed · ✓ supported
same inputs, same verdict, every replay — auditable by design.

reverse

The semantic faithfulness judge

It works backward from the prose. Each written sentence is tested for entailment against the data row by a cross-family model — a different model lineage than the one that wrote the draft, so a writer's blind spot isn't also the checker's. A sentence the data can't entail is flagged, even when no claim was formally declared.

sentence "live by Friday" → entailment fails · ✗ unsupported
this is the check generic guardrails miss.

04 — Why it's safe

The model is the writer, never the decider.

We don't claim no one else automates outreach. We claim something narrower and provable: we verify the AI's claims against the source of truth, in both directions, before a human approves a single send.

Deterministic where it counts

The risk score and the blocker come from code, not a model. Same inputs, same output, every time — replayable, and defensible.

Every claim traces to a field

The forward gatekeeper ties each declared claim to the merchant's own record. If the data doesn't back it, the claim doesn't ship.

Every sentence read for entailment

The reverse semantic judge, on a cross-family model, catches true-sounding prose the data can't support — the fabrications generic guardrails miss.

A human owns the send

The one irreversible action always belongs to a person. The system holds; it never sends on its own. Hold, edit, or send.

Spend is hard-capped

A $5 cost ledger fails closed — it halts the run before it can overspend. The public demo is replay-only: no live calls.

A full audit trail

Every step, every verdict, every decision is recorded. The run can be reconstructed end to end — by an auditor, not just an engineer.

05 — Why it generalizes

The verification spine outlives the use case.

Stalled-merchant activation is the worked example. The shape underneath it — prove every claim forward, read every sentence backward, keep a human on the trigger — is a pattern for any time you let AI write to people at scale.

data row→ bounded draft→ claims-gate→ faithfulness judge→ human approval

Swap the merchants for patients, borrowers, students, or subscribers and the discipline holds: let code make the risky call, let the model write within bounds, prove every claim against the source of truth in both directions, and put a person on the send. That is the transferable part — and the reason this is built to be adopted, not just demoed.
