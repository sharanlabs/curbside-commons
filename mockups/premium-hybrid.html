The lost revenue nobody works

A marketplace's signed-up-but-never-live merchants are real GMV, leaking quietly.

The long tail is too big to work by hand. Merchants stall somewhere between sign-up and first order — and the outreach that reaches them has to be both matched to the blocker and true to the record.

01

The long tail is too big to work by hand

Thousands of pre-live merchants, a handful of activation reps. Most never get a tailored touch.

02

Generic outreach is the #1 failure

A blast that ignores the actual blocker reads as spam. Matched-to-the-blocker is what converts.

03

And AI will confidently make things up

Let a model write at scale and it invents dates, offers, and statuses. Generic guardrails miss claims that are merely unsupported — not toxic.

The pipeline

From stalled merchant to a verified, human-approved message.

Risk is decided by replayable code. The model writes inside a bounded contract. Then two verification passes — forward and reverse — run before a human ever sees the draft.

Auto-advancing — click any step to explore.
Step 01 · Deterministic

Triage

Find who's stuck — and exactly how badly. A transparent risk score and the single blocking step, every time.

HowA deterministic risk score plus the blocker, from an auditable formula. No model touches this call — it's replayable and identical on every run.
Output: risk level + blocker
Step 02 · Deterministic

Diagnose

Read the engagement state, name the root cause, and pick a reactivation play that varies by why they're stuck — not just by which step they're on.

HowEngagement state from recency × steps × tenure; merchant-vs-platform blocker source; a play matched to the cause, not a one-size message.
Output: root cause + play
Step 03 · Bounded LLM

Draft

Now — and only now — a model writes the message. It works inside a schema-constrained contract and declares every claim it makes.

HowA bounded LLM produces structured output with a typed claims array. The untrusted merchant name never enters the prompt — it's substituted after gating.
Output: draft + declared claims
Step 04 · Claims gatekeeper · forward

Gate

Every declared claim is checked against the merchant's own data — claim → data — and against forbidden-claim guardrails. Deterministic, so it runs the same way every time.

HowEach declared claim must trace to a field on the merchant row; forbidden claims are blocked outright. Anything unverifiable is held, not sent.
Output: pass / hold / block
Step 05 · Faithfulness judge · reverse

Judge

The gate checks what the model declared. The judge checks what it actually wrote: every prose sentence must be entailed by the data row — so an undeclared fabrication can't slip through.

HowA calibrated semantic judge runs sentence → data entailment with a cross-family model (a different family than the drafter). Maker isn't judge — an independent reader scores faithfulness.
Output: per-sentence faithfulness
Step 06 · Human-in-the-loop

Approve

A person makes the final call — hold, reject, or send. Every decision lands in a full audit trail, beside a fail-closed cost ledger.

HowThe human owns the irreversible action. Sends are simulated in this demo; spend is hard-capped at $5 and recorded line by line.
Output: decision + audit record
The catch in action

When the model invents a promise, the judge holds it.

Why a marketplace exec can trust it

A direct antidote to the AI-outreach failure frontier.

False claims, churn from spammy blasts, regulatory liability — the known failure modes of "let AI message customers." This system is designed against each one, in two directions.

Deterministic where it counts

The risky call — who's at risk and why — is made by code, not a model. Same inputs, same output, every replay.

Replayable by design

Forward gate: every declared claim → data

The gatekeeper checks each claim against the merchant's own record, and blocks forbidden claims outright. Deterministic and replayable.

No unverifiable promises

Reverse judge: every sentence → entailment

A calibrated faithfulness judge reads the prose back against the row, sentence by sentence — catching the undeclared fabrications a forward check can't see.

Semantic faithfulness, scored

Maker isn't judge — cross-family

The model that wrote the draft does not grade it. A different model family reads it independently, so a writer's blind spots aren't its own reviewer.

Independent reviewer

A human owns every irreversible send

Nothing leaves the system without a person choosing to send it. The default is to hold, not to act.

Human-in-the-loop gate

Spend hard-capped, demo replay-only

A fail-closed $5 cost ledger records every model call and halts before it overspends. The public demo makes no live calls — it replays a recorded run.

Why it generalizes

The same spine works far beyond merchant activation.

Deterministic triage → bounded LLM → forward claims-gate → reverse faithfulness judge → human approval → audit. Any problem shaped like "use AI to act on people or accounts at scale, safely" can stand on it.

Collections & dunning

Onboarding & KYC nudges
