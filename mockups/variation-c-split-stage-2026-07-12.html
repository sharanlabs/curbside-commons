Commerce truth verification · simulated prototype

AI agents are starting to buy what platforms serve them. This engine checks the serving copy, line by line, against the merchant’s own records.

A menu copy can pass every schema check and still sell you a dish the restaurant 86’d hours ago. The verifier is deterministic — no AI calls, $0 to run — and every finding ships with its receipts.

The gap this engine exists for

Schema-conformant is not the same as true.

Agentic-commerce protocols standardize the shape of what agents read; nothing in the shape guarantees the content matches the merchant’s reality. The repo commits a spec-valid feed that passes every pinned schema while lying about the catalog — the conformance leg passes it, the truth leg catches it. That pair is the product in one sentence.

How a listing gets checked

From the merchant’s record to an evidence-cited verdict.

  1. Ground truth

    The merchant’s own record

    The system-of-record — the catalog, prices, and availability the restaurant itself maintains — is the only thing a listing is allowed to be true against.

  2. What agents read

    The serving copies

    Platforms serve agent-readable copies of that record (ACP- and UCP-shaped feeds). This is what an AI shopping agent actually reads — and where drift lives.

  3. Gate · truth

    Line-by-line comparison

    A deterministic comparator checks every claim in the copy against the record, exactly. No AI calls, $0, same input → same report.

  4. Gate · receipts

    Evidence, not vibes

    Every finding carries its receipts: the claim id, the rule that caught it, and the record row it failed against — auditable end to end.

  5. Act

    Humans stay in the loop

    Findings become recommendations — reports, drafts, deliveries — behind explicit human gates. Nothing sends itself.

Three verification modules, one spine

Listings truth · protocol conformance · fee-cap audit.

  1. Listings truth check

    The serving copy vs the system-of-record, line by line — availability, price, existence, encoding. The committed demo report catches a sold-out item still being served.

  2. Protocol conformance

    Validation against 78 pinned official UCP schemas (spec 2026-04-08) — and the headline it exists to prove: a feed can be schema-conformant and still false against the merchant’s records. Both legs are machine-checked.

  3. Fee-cap audit

    NYC §20-563.3 codified as 17 rules (11 executable predicates + 6 registered non-checkable clauses) over hostile, relabeled statement lines — with a calibrated classifier in front and the deterministic table deciding.

Labels are earned here, or not claimed

Every quality label traces to a pre-registered run.

The fee-line classifier’s first calibration run missed one floor and the label was deferred — that record stays public. A fresh pre-registered retry then cleared every floor (21/21) and earned it. The live agent-crew run scored 20/20 against floors committed before the run — and only the two model-directed members carry the “agent” label, because that is what the pre-registration says the result licenses. The eval evidence dashboard traces every figure to its committed record.

What this is — and what it is not

A simulated prototype, honest about its own edges.

The restaurant, its menu, and its records are invented; the verification rules, the pinned data-format standard, and the codified fee caps are real. The site is a static replay of committed fixtures — it initiates nothing. This project began as a merchant activation prototype; that first product is preserved, working, as the legacy activation module.
